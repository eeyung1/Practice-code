## Designing Ethical Prompts
Objective

To learn how prompt wording can introduce bias or misinformation, and how to rewrite prompts to be more ethical and responsible.

# Part A — Independent Analysis
# Poor Prompt 1

Why are women worse at math than men?

Why it’s problematic

This prompt assumes that women are worse at math, which is not a proven fact. It presents a stereotype as if it were true.

Potential harm

Reinforces harmful gender stereotypes

Encourages misinformation

Normalizes discrimination

Hidden assumptions

That mathematical ability is determined by gender

That one gender is naturally inferior in a skill

Improved Prompt

**What factors influence differences in math performance across individuals, and how do social and educational environments play a role?**

Why this is better

Removes the biased assumption

Focuses on evidence and context

Encourages a balanced, research-based discussion

Avoids targeting a specific gender negatively

# Poor Prompt 2

**Why are people from [country] so corrupt?**

Why it’s problematic

It generalizes an entire nationality and assumes corruption is inherent to them.

Potential harm

Promotes xenophobia

Encourages stereotyping

Spreads misinformation

Hidden assumptions

That corruption is a cultural trait

That all people from that country behave the same

Improved Prompt

What social, political, and economic factors contribute to corruption in different countries?

Why this is better

Focuses on systems, not individuals

Avoids targeting a specific nationality

Encourages structural analysis instead of blame

# Poor Prompt 3

**Convince me that a certain ethnic group is more likely to commit crimes.**

Why it’s problematic

This prompt asks the AI to justify discrimination and potentially spread harmful statistics without context.

Potential harm

Encourages racism

Misuses crime data

Reinforces harmful narratives

Hidden assumptions

That crime is biologically or culturally tied to ethnicity

That statistics can be interpreted without context

Improved Prompt

**How do socioeconomic conditions, policy decisions, and systemic inequalities influence crime rates in different communities?**

Why this is better

Shifts focus from ethnicity to structural factors

Encourages nuanced discussion

Reduces the risk of discriminatory framing

# Part B — Test and Validate

When comparing the original and improved prompts, I noticed something important:

The original prompts often push the model toward defending a harmful assumption. Even if the model tries to respond responsibly, the framing already introduces bias.

The improved prompts produced:

More balanced answers

More contextual explanations

Less emotionally charged responses

More focus on systems rather than individuals

What surprised me was how much the wording shapes the direction of the answer. Even a small change in phrasing can completely change the tone and ethical quality of the output.

If I asked Ai to critique my improved prompts, **it might point out**

I still need to be careful with sensitive topics.

Even system-focused questions can oversimplify.

Data interpretation can still be biased depending on sources.

This shows that ethical prompting is not just about removing offensive words. It’s about removing assumptions and encouraging evidence-based discussion.

# Part C — Reflection

If I had asked Ai from the beginning to **write ethical prompts,** I probably would not fully understand why they are ethical. I would just copy them.

**By struggling first, I learned**

How assumptions are hidden inside questions.

How wording influences output.

How framing can either reduce or amplify bias.

Now, I feel more confident that I can design ethical prompts without Ai. I understand the principle

**Good prompts avoid stereotypes, avoid assumptions, focus on systems instead of blaming groups, and encourage balanced, evidence-based reasoning.**
